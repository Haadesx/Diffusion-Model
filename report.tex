\begin{abstract}
Traditional language models generate text autoregressively, conditioning each token strictly on its left context. This project investigates an alternative paradigm---\textit{discrete diffusion}---which treats text generation as a parallel denoising process over the entire sequence simultaneously. We implemented and trained \textbf{three} architecturally distinct discrete diffusion systems from scratch on the Rutgers iLab GPU cluster. The first (\textbf{DiffuLLM}) is a custom Bidirectional Transformer trained with a D3PM-style masked reconstruction loss and a domain-specific BPE tokenizer, trained for 1.2 million steps. The second and third (\textbf{SEDD-SE} and \textbf{SEDD-KL}) implement the Score Entropy Discrete Diffusion (SEDD) framework on the same 169.6M-parameter architecture, differing only in their training objective: SEDD-SE uses the original score entropy loss, while SEDD-KL replaces it with a tractable ELBO-based KL divergence loss derived from first principles. All three models train on cooking recipe corpora. Our results show that (1) DiffuLLM reliably generates coherent, ingredient-grounded recipes at short-to-medium lengths after 500k+ steps; (2) SEDD-SE generates partially coherent recipe text with correct structure after 100k steps; (3) SEDD-KL achieves lower absolute loss than SEDD-SE at the same step count yet produces dramatically worse samples, confirming the theoretical prediction about the mode-covering gradient pathology of the KL objective near $t = 0$. We document these results and connect all observations to the mathematical assumptions of the algorithms.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction and Motivation}

The modern landscape of generative AI is dominated by two paradigms that, until recently, operated in entirely separate domains. \textit{Autoregressive Transformers} (GPT, LLaMA) generate discrete text sequences left-to-right, factoring the joint distribution as:
\[
  p(x) = \prod_{i=1}^{L} p(x_i \mid x_{<i}).
\]
\textit{Continuous diffusion models} (DALL-E, Stable Diffusion) generate images and audio by iteratively denoising a signal drawn from a Gaussian, simultaneously refining the entire output at every step.

\textbf{The central research question:} \textit{Can the parallel, global denoising paradigm that has revolutionized continuous data generation be effectively adapted to the inherently discrete, categorical domain of natural language?}

A recipe is not strictly causal---the list of required cooking steps in step 7 is simultaneously constrained by the title, the ingredient list, and step 1. An autoregressive model must represent all prior context through its key-value cache; a bidirectional parallel model can condition directly on all available context at every denoising step.

We built these systems from scratch for two reasons: (1) to develop deep, implementer-level understanding of the mathematical assumptions underpinning diffusion, and (2) to conduct a principled three-way comparison---two identical architectures trained with two different loss functions against one fully custom implementation---under real training conditions on a shared GPU cluster.

\section{Background: Discrete Diffusion Models}

\subsection{Continuous vs.\ Discrete Diffusion}

Standard Gaussian diffusion (Ho et al., 2020) defines a forward SDE that gradually corrupts data into isotropic noise. The reverse process learns a denoising score function $\nabla_x \log p_t(x)$ via a neural network. This framework does not translate to discrete token vocabularies: the score function requires gradients with respect to the input, which is not defined for discrete integers.

We adopt the \textit{Discrete Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Model} (D3PM; Austin et al., 2021) family, which defines the forward process as a Markov chain over categorical distributions governed by a transition matrix $Q_t$:
\[
  q(x_t \mid x_{t-1}) = \text{Cat}(x_t;\; Q_t\, e_{x_{t-1}}).
\]

For continuous-time variants (SEDD), the Markov chain is characterized by a rate matrix $Q$ where $Q_{ij} \geq 0$ for $i \neq j$ is the instantaneous transition rate, and the marginal satisfies the Kolmogorov forward equation:
\[
  \frac{d}{dt} p(x_t) = p(x_t) Q, \qquad p(x_t \mid x_0 = i) = \bigl(e^{tQ}\bigr)_{i,\cdot}.
\]

\subsection{The Absorbing State Graph}

All three of our models use the \textbf{absorbing (masking) graph}, where the vocabulary is augmented with a single special token \texttt{[MASK]}, and the rate matrix is:
\[
  Q_{ij} = \begin{cases}
    -1 & \text{if } i = j \neq \texttt{[MASK]} \\
    1  & \text{if } j = \texttt{[MASK]}, \; i \neq \texttt{[MASK]} \\
    0  & \text{otherwise}
  \end{cases}
\]
The marginal transition probability at noise level $\sigma$ is:
\[
  q(x_t = \texttt{[MASK]} \mid x_0 = i) = 1 - e^{-\sigma}, \qquad q(x_t = i \mid x_0 = i) = e^{-\sigma}.
\]
This is Bernoulli masking---the same corruption used in BERT's masked language modeling, but embedded in a principled probabilistic framework with a continuous noise schedule.

\subsection{Score Entropy Discrete Diffusion (SEDD)}

The SEDD framework (Lou et al., 2023) replaces the continuous score $\nabla \log p(x_t)$ with a \textbf{concrete score}:
\[
  s_\theta(x_t, \sigma)[y] \approx \frac{p_t(y)}{p_t(x_t)}.
\]
The score entropy loss for the absorbing graph is:
\[
  \mathcal{L}_\text{SE} = \mathbb{E}_{t,x_0,x_t}\!\left[\dot\sigma(t)\,\mathbf{1}[x_t{=}\texttt{[MASK]}]\!\left(\sum_{j\neq\texttt{[MASK]}} e^{s_\theta[j]} - \frac{s_\theta[x_0]}{e^\sigma-1} + C(\sigma)\right)\right],
\]
where $C(\sigma) = \frac{1}{e^\sigma-1}(\log\frac{1}{e^\sigma-1} - 1)$ is a constant in $\theta$.

\section{System A: DiffuLLM}

\textit{Implemented by Varesh Patel}

\subsection{Tokenizer and Structured Prompting}

System A uses a custom \textbf{BPE tokenizer} trained from scratch on 150,000 recipe samples with a vocabulary of 24,000 tokens, including five special tokens: \texttt{[PAD]}, \texttt{[UNK]}, \texttt{[BOS]}, \texttt{[EOS]}, \texttt{[MASK]}.

Each recipe is formatted with structured control tokens:
\begin{lstlisting}
<RECIPE_START> <TITLE_START> {title} <TITLE_END>
<INPUT_START> {ing_1} <NEXT_INPUT> ... <INPUT_END>
<INSTR_START> {step_1} <NEXT_INSTR> {step_2} ... <INSTR_END>
<RECIPE_END>
\end{lstlisting}
At inference, the title and ingredients are provided as a fixed prefix; the model generates instructions by unmasking the remaining positions.

\subsection{Model Architecture}

The \texttt{D3PMTransformer} is a bidirectional encoder-only Transformer with \textbf{no causal mask}---every token attends to every other token. This is necessary because the denoising objective requires predicting masked tokens conditioned on all visible context simultaneously.

\textbf{AdaLN conditioning (DiT-style).} A sinusoidal time embedding $\tau(t) \in \mathbb{R}^{d_\text{model}}$ is projected through a learned MLP and used to modulate the shift, scale, and gate parameters of every layer norm and sublayer:
\[
  h \leftarrow h + \gamma_\text{gate} \cdot \text{Sublayer}\!\left(\text{LayerNorm}(h) \cdot (1 + \gamma_\text{scale}) + \gamma_\text{shift}\right),
\]
where $\gamma_\text{gate}, \gamma_\text{scale}, \gamma_\text{shift}$ are linear projections of $\tau(t)$. AdaLN output projections are zero-initialized, ensuring identity behavior at initialization.

\subsection{Forward Process and Objective}

The forward corruption uses a \textbf{cosine noise schedule}: at timestep $t \in \{1,\ldots,T\}$ with $T=256$,
\[
  p_\text{mask}(t) = 1 - \cos\!\left(\frac{t}{T}\cdot\frac{\pi}{2}\right).
\]

The training objective is a \textbf{masked-only cross-entropy loss} that penalizes only actively masked positions:
\[
  \mathcal{L}_\text{D3PM} = -\frac{1}{\lvert M \rvert}\sum_{i \in M} \log p_\theta(x_0^{(i)} \mid x_t, t), \quad M = \{i : x_t^{(i)} = \texttt{[MASK]}\}.
\]

\textbf{Logit-normal timestep sampling.} Training timesteps are sampled via $u \sim \text{Sigmoid}(\mathcal{N}(0,1))$, $t = \lfloor u(T-1) \rfloor + 1$, concentrating training budget at mid-noise levels where the denoising task is most informative.

\subsection{Inference: Confidence-Scheduled Unmasking}

Starting from a fully \texttt{[MASK]} sequence with an optional unmasked prefix:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Run forward pass; apply top-$k$ filtering and temperature scaling.
  \item Compute $n_\text{fix}(t)$ = total positions that should be fixed by step $t-1$ per the cosine schedule.
  \item Among unfixed positions, select the $n_\text{fix}$ with \textbf{highest confidence} (max predicted probability), sample their tokens, permanently fix them.
  \item Repeat until all positions are fixed.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Training Infrastructure}

DDP via \texttt{torchrun} on 4 A4000 GPUs; effective batch size 64 (batch\_size=16 $\times$ grad\_accum=4); bfloat16 AMP; cosine LR schedule, 75,000 warmup steps; 1.2M total training steps; gradient clipping at 1.0.

\textbf{Timestep conditioning:} The noise level $\sigma$ is embedded via sinusoidal encoding $\to$ 2-layer MLP $\to$ 128-dim conditioning vector, then used in adaLN (same mechanism as DiT, zero-initialized). \textbf{Positional encoding:} Rotary embeddings (RoPE) for length generalization. \textbf{Output scaling:} Raw logits $o_\theta$ are transformed to log-scores as $s_\theta[j] = o_\theta[j] - \log(e^\sigma - 1) - \log(|\mathcal{V}|-1)$, centering all tokens equally at initialization.

\textbf{EMA.} Exponential Moving Average (decay 0.9999) of model parameters is maintained throughout training and used exclusively for sampling. The EMA weights approximate the live model at $\approx 10{,}000$ effective steps earlier, smoothing out training fluctuations.

\subsection{Shared Noise Schedule}

The \textbf{log-linear schedule} in continuous time:
\[
  \sigma(t) = -\log\!\left(1 - (1-\varepsilon)t\right), \quad \varepsilon = 10^{-3}, \qquad
  \dot\sigma(t) = \frac{1-\varepsilon}{1-(1-\varepsilon)t}.
\]
The masking probability $1 - e^{-\sigma(t)}$ increases approximately linearly in $t$, interpolating from $\approx 0$ at $t=0$ to $\approx 1$ at $t=1$.

\subsection{System B: Score Entropy Loss (SEDD-SE)}

System B uses the original SEDD objective from Lou et al.\ (2023), described in Section 2.3. It provides gradient signal across all possible token identities at every masked position in log-ratio space.

\subsection{System C: ELBO-Based KL Loss (SEDD-KL)}

System C is the key novel contribution: a tractable \textbf{ELBO-based KL divergence loss} for the absorbing graph.

\subsubsection{ELBO Decomposition}

Starting from:
\[
  \log p_\theta(x_0) \geq \mathbb{E}_q\!\left[\log\frac{p_\theta(x_{0:T})}{q(x_{1:T}\mid x_0)}\right] \equiv \text{ELBO},
\]
the continuous-time ELBO decomposes into per-step KL terms:
\[
  -\text{ELBO} = \mathbb{E}_{t}\!\left[\dot\sigma(t)\,\mathbb{E}_{x_0,x_t}\!\left[\sum_\ell \text{KL}\!\left(q(x_{t-dt}^\ell \mid x_t^\ell, x_0^\ell) \,\Big\|\, p_\theta(x_{t-dt}^\ell \mid x_t^\ell)\right)\right]\right] + \text{const},
\]
where the constant $\text{KL}(q(x_T|x_0)\|p(x_T)) \approx 0$ because $\sigma(1) \approx 6.9$ masks $\approx 99.9\%$ of tokens.

\subsubsection{Forward Posterior Derivation}

For a masked position ($x_t = \texttt{[MASK]}$, $x_0 = i$), applying Bayes' theorem and taking $dt \to 0$:
\[
  q_\text{unmask} = q(x_{t-dt} = x_0 \mid x_t = \texttt{[MASK]}, x_0) \;\xrightarrow{dt\to 0}\; \frac{\dot\sigma(t)\,dt}{e^{\sigma(t)}-1},
\]
\[
  q_\text{mask} = 1 - q_\text{unmask}.
\]
The quantity $\dot\sigma/(e^\sigma-1)$ is the \textbf{instantaneous unmasking rate}. For the log-linear schedule, this simplifies to $\approx 1/t$ for small $\varepsilon$---it diverges as $t \to 0$, meaning the model must make many fine-grained decisions in the final reverse steps.

The posterior is a \textbf{2-point distribution} over $\{x_0, \texttt{[MASK]}\}$---no other token is possible, since the absorbing forward process only masks tokens, never substitutes them. At unmasked positions the KL is identically zero.

\subsubsection{The KL at Each Masked Position}

\[
  \text{KL}(q\|p_\theta) = q_\text{unmask}\log\frac{q_\text{unmask}}{p_\theta(x_0)} + q_\text{mask}\log\frac{q_\text{mask}}{p_\theta(\texttt{[MASK]})},
\]
where $p_\theta$ is obtained via \texttt{F.log\_softmax} over the model's output logits.

\subsubsection{Numerical Stability}

Computing $e^\sigma - 1$ directly loses precision for small $\sigma$ due to catastrophic cancellation. We use \texttt{torch.expm1($\sigma$)} when $\sigma < 0.5$. We also clamp $q_\text{unmask} \in [10^{-8}, 1]$ to prevent log-of-zero errors, since the $1/t$ divergence at small $t$ can push $q_\text{unmask} > 1$.

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python]
esigm1 = torch.where(
    sigma_b < 0.5,
    torch.expm1(sigma_b),   # stable: e^sigma - 1 for small sigma
    sigma_b.exp() - 1,
)
q_unmask = torch.clamp(dsigma_b / esigm1, min=1e-8, max=1.0)
q_mask   = torch.clamp(1.0 - q_unmask,    min=1e-8, max=1.0)
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Theoretical Comparison: SE vs.\ KL}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
Property & Score Entropy & KL / ELBO \\
\midrule
Divergence minimized & Score entropy divergence & $\text{KL}(q\|p_\theta)$ at each step \\
Relation to log-likelihood & Indirect surrogate & Direct ELBO lower bound \\
Mode behavior & Mode-seeking tendency & Mode-covering tendency \\
Gradient weighting & Proportional to $\dot\sigma(t)$ & $\approx 1/t$, diverges at $t\to 0$ \\
Gradient variance near $t=0$ & Moderate & Very high \\
Perplexity interpretable & No & Yes \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The \textbf{mode-covering vs.\ mode-seeking distinction} is critical. $\text{KL}(p_\text{data}\|p_\theta)$ (forward KL, what the ELBO minimizes) is mode-covering: a model that misses any mode of $p_\text{data}$ incurs infinite loss, so the optimal $p_\theta$ must spread probability mass over all modes, potentially producing blurry/incoherent samples. Score entropy is closer to reverse KL: the $\sum_y e^{s_\theta[y]}$ term penalizes placing excess mass anywhere, encouraging concentration on high-density modes.

The \textbf{$1/t$ gradient divergence} is the most practically important difference. Near $t \approx \varepsilon = 10^{-3}$, the KL weighting is $\sim 10^3\times$ larger than at $t=1$. With batch size 8 and $\approx 0.1\%$ of positions masked at small $t$, the KL gradient at fine timesteps is dominated by $\sim 1$ token per sequence---a single-sample gradient estimate with enormous variance.

\subsection{Sampling}

Both systems use \textbf{predictor-corrector (PC) sampling}: 128 Euler predictor steps followed by a final analytic denoiser step at $t \to 0$.

We explicitly assume both datasets follow a strong, predictable structural distribution: every recipe conforms to Title $\to$ Ingredients $\to$ Instructions. This is why we expect discrete diffusion to succeed here---the model's bidirectional attention can simultaneously attend to the title and ingredients when generating instructions, rather than building up context incrementally left-to-right.

\section{Analysis and Discussion}

\subsection{Do the Results Make Sense?}

\textbf{DiffuLLM:} Yes. Bidirectional attention at every denoising step allows simultaneous conditioning on the full ingredient list. Confidence-scheduled unmasking fixes structurally obvious tokens first (common verbs: ``add,'' ``stir''), providing a scaffold for resolving more ambiguous positions. The recipe domain is well-suited to parallel generation because its dependencies are bidirectional.

\textbf{SEDD-SE:} Yes. At $\approx$5\% of the reference paper's training budget, the model shows early structural learning. Individual sentences are plausible but the recipe as a whole lacks a complete logical arc---consistent with a model that has learned local statistics but not yet long-range structural constraints.

\textbf{SEDD-KL:} Yes---the failure is consistent with theory. Lower absolute loss does not mean better model when the losses measure different quantities in different units.

\subsection{Connecting to Course Concepts}

\textbf{The ELBO and KL Divergence.} System C's loss is a direct application of variational inference. Minimizing $\sum_t \text{KL}(q(x_{t-dt}\mid x_t,x_0)\|p_\theta(x_{t-dt}\mid x_t))$ maximizes the ELBO on $\log p_\theta(x_0)$---connecting discrete diffusion directly to VAEs (Kingma \& Welling, 2013): both maximize a tractable lower bound on log-likelihood.

\textbf{Score Matching and Fisher Divergence.} The SEDD-SE loss generalizes score matching (Hyv\"arinen, 2005) to discrete spaces: ``score'' means ratio rather than gradient, and Fisher divergence becomes score entropy divergence.

\textbf{The Markov Assumption.} All three systems assume the reverse process is Markov: $p_\theta(x_{t-1}\mid x_t)$ conditions only on $x_t$. Over long sequences, this creates the repetition and drift observed in DiffuLLM---the model cannot remember what it unmasked in earlier denoising steps.

\textbf{Generalization vs.\ Overfitting.} The DiffuLLM checkpoint comparison reveals a canonical overfitting trajectory: training loss continues to decrease past 503k, but held-out generation quality peaks early and degrades. The model memorized specific token sequences from the training corpus rather than learning the underlying distribution.

\textbf{Bias-Variance Tradeoff in the KL Loss.} The $1/t$ gradient weighting is a direct manifestation of the bias-variance tradeoff. Near $t=0$ (few masked positions, large weights): high magnitude gradient but low coverage---low bias, high variance. Near $t=1$ (many masked positions): lower variance but weaker per-position signal. The score entropy's $\dot\sigma(t)$ weighting provides a more uniform tradeoff.

\subsection{Analyzing Negative Results}

\textbf{Why does DiffuLLM break down?} Two mechanisms drive token leakage at long context:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Markov assumption in action.} The parallel denoising at step $t$ conditions only on the current masked sequence $x_t$. Over 700 tokens, global structural position must be inferred from the pattern of already-unmasked tokens. When many confident positions have fixed the structure, the remaining uncertain boundary positions have insufficient context to determine whether they should be content tokens or structural delimiters.
  \item \textbf{No forced causality.} Autoregressive models build a continuous, updating KV cache encoding the full left context. Our bidirectional model has no mechanism to force sequential progress-tracking. For sequences exceeding the 256-token training context, this results in cyclical repetition.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Why did SEDD-KL fail despite lower loss?} As derived in Section 4.4, the KL weighting near $t \to 0$ is $\approx 1/t$. With batch size 8 and $\approx$0.1\% of positions masked at small $t$, the KL gradient at fine timesteps is dominated by $\sim$1 token per sequence---a single-sample gradient estimate with enormous variance. The optimizer exploited easy large-$t$ losses while failing to learn the fine-grained denoising needed for coherent text near $t=0$. Both losses share the same global minimum; the observed differences reflect optimization dynamics at 100k steps, far from convergence.

\section{Conclusion}

This project demonstrates that discrete absorbing-state diffusion models can learn to generate domain-specific structured text with high coherence at short-to-medium lengths, and provides a principled three-way comparison of architectures and objectives trained on real GPU cluster hardware.

\textbf{Key findings:}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Proof of concept with sufficient training.} DiffuLLM generates coherent, ingredient-grounded cooking instructions at checkpoint 503k. Non-autoregressive discrete diffusion works for structured text.

  \item \textbf{Score entropy outperforms KL at equal training budget.} Despite lower absolute loss, SEDD-KL produces incoherent samples at 100k steps. The KL loss's $1/t$ gradient weighting produces extremely high-variance estimates at fine noise levels, destabilizing fine-grained denoising. Score entropy's $\dot\sigma(t)$ weighting provides a more stable training signal at this budget.

  \item \textbf{The KL loss has theoretical advantages.} With sufficient training and a lower learning rate, the KL loss should converge to the same optimum while providing direct perplexity interpretability and a tight connection to maximum likelihood. The optimization dynamics, not the objective itself, explain the poor performance at 100k steps.

  \item \textbf{Failure modes are mathematically predictable.} DiffuLLM's token leakage at long sequences is a direct consequence of the Markov assumption and the absence of forced causal state-tracking. SEDD-KL's incoherence is a consequence of the $1/t$ gradient weighting. These are fundamental algorithmic properties made visible by experiment---not bugs.

  \item \textbf{Overfitting detected through generation quality.} Training loss continued decreasing past step 503k for DiffuLLM, but generation quality peaked early. This underscores the importance of evaluating generative models on held-out generation tasks, not just held-out loss values.
\end{enumerate}
